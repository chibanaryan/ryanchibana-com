There is a kind of analysis I was trained to do, and another kind I was not.

In computer science you are taught, very early, to think carefully about complexity. Time complexity, space complexity: the way the resource demands of a procedure grow as the size of the problem grows. You learn to say of an algorithm that it runs in linear time, or quadratic, or exponential. You learn that one approach is asymptotically better than another. The analysis becomes a precondition for serious work. The inability or unwillingness to perform it is a marker of an unserious technologist.

There is another kind of complexity I think we should be analyzing with the same rigor and are not. The moral complexity of the systems we build. The institutions whose responsiveness they presuppose. The capacities they exercise and the ones they atrophy. The question of who absorbs the friction. We treat this as soft, optional, the domain of ethics committees, the thing you say before you get to the technical content. I think it is one of the costliest deficiencies in our discipline, and the source of a great deal of harm we are individually unable to perceive and collectively unable to prevent.

Taking this complexity seriously is a capacity I have been trying to develop. The unexamined vocation is not worth living.

## The car that did not stop

Some moments break through. The first time I felt, with any force, the gap between the technical analysis I was good at and the moral analysis I was not, I was at my desk reading what had happened at [Cruise](https://sfstandard.com/2024/11/14/cruise-fine-investigation-dragging-robotaxi/).

A pedestrian had been hit by a hit-and-run driver in San Francisco and thrown into the path of a Cruise robotaxi, which dragged her some distance before stopping. The reporting that followed made clear that the company's failures, which led to a suspended permit and eventually to the unwinding of the entire program, were as much about what was disclosed to regulators as about what had happened on the street. I had worked there, on the ML data pipelines for the trajectory planning model. By any narrow technical accounting, I was at a remove from the decisions in question. And still I sat with the news in a way I had not sat with any work-related news before, sick to my stomach, unable to focus in the days and even weeks that followed.

The question I have been asking myself ever since is not whether I bear specific causal responsibility for what happened. I don't think I do. The question is whether I was deficient in perceiving the morally relevant aspects of the situation while I was inside it. Not the specific incident. I could not have anticipated that. The broader shape. The financial pressure the company was under, the race to a deployable product, the kinds of corner-cutting that get rationalized inside a company on a financial lifeline. I can produce, in retrospect, all the reasons I was technically removed from any of it. I did not work on operations. I did not interact with regulators. I built infrastructure that processed sensor data. The reasons are true. They are also a kind of accounting I do not entirely trust.

I felt the speed. We had weeks, at one point, to scale the volume of data the trajectory ranking model could handle by an order of magnitude. A line from Tolkien kept coming back to me: the quest stands upon the edge of a knife. I thought about that pressure as a question of personal sacrifice, of what I would have to give up to meet the deadlines. What I did not have the cognitive space for, inside it, was the way that same pressure, on the operational side of the company, could be producing the third-party harms the safety case for the technology was supposed to prevent. The race to profitability does not stay on its own side of the company.

I lent my skills, and pieces of my life that could have gone elsewhere, to an institution whose precariousness was driving choices I either did not see or did not let myself see. To collapse this into either "I was implicated" or "I was not" would be a violation of what I owe the question. I am uncertain how much of the seeing was available to me at the time.

What Cruise shifted in me was not a position on self-driving. The technology itself is genuinely complex, even setting aside the specific failures. The case for it is real. Fewer drunk drivers, fewer drowsy ones, the possibility of giving a disabled person who cannot drive the freedom to leave their house alone. The case against is also real, and not only in the worst-case sense. There were the traffic backups that cascaded through San Francisco neighborhoods. The labor implications for taxi and rideshare drivers. The way a city became, without quite consenting to it, the test environment for a technology whose third-party costs the city would absorb. The reasonable response is not to land cleanly on one side. The reasonable response is to keep all of this in view at once while still saying something.

What Cruise shifted in me was something more like the question itself: whether I had been bringing the same intellectual rigor to the moral dimensions of my work that I had been bringing to its technical dimensions. The honest answer was that I had not. The discomfort of that answer is part of what I am holding.

## Three doors and a fourth

Albert Hirschman gave us a vocabulary for what a person does when an institution they care about begins to fail. [*Exit, Voice, and Loyalty*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674276604) is famously elegant: you can leave, you can speak up, or you can hang in there. The taxonomy is clean. The choice is not. Hirschman's actual argument is that the three options interact. Loyalty is what makes voice possible: if you are not going to leave, you have skin in the game to speak up, and your speaking has weight. Easy exit weakens voice, because the dissatisfied just leave instead of fighting. Later scholars added a less honorable fourth: neglect, the staying without engaging, going through motions while withholding the part of yourself that might have made a difference.

I have done all of these in different proportions at different employers, and the proportions did not reliably track the moral stakes. There were periods when I should have exercised more voice and chose a low-engagement form of loyalty instead. There were periods when I exercised voice and was not sure whether it was changing anything. There were exits I chose well and exits I chose late. The framework is useful precisely because it forces the question that the lived experience does not always pose clearly: what am I doing here, right now, with respect to this institution? Am I shaping it, leaving it, deepening my commitment, or pretending to one of these while doing another?

## The structural undervaluing

I worked at Block for the last year or so on the compliance engineering side. The job, as I understood it, was to put friction where the institution would not otherwise put it: to detect and remediate gaps in the program, to track the data that audits depended on, to push for what regulators would eventually insist on whether we volunteered it or not. It meant swimming against the flow of teams whose incentives ran in a different direction. This was voice work, in Hirschman's vocabulary: using a position inside the institution to push for things the institution would not otherwise reliably do.

In practice, much of what I did was instrument compliance: build the systems that would tell us, in close to real time, whether we were meeting our obligations or drifting out of them. The technical work was straightforward. The political work, getting other teams to allocate even a small slice of their roadmap to the instrumentation, was the harder part. The pattern repeated: "we are reworking that system," "now is not a good time," "we will get to it next quarter." Now never arrived. I was getting "this is worth doing" from my management chain and "this is not worth our time" from everyone else, even when I designed the work to make the smallest possible demand on the teams whose cooperation I needed. The company was aware enough of its compliance gaps to fund a team to fill them in. It was not motivated enough about them for the rest of the organization to make the bare minimum space and time to execute.

The public record on the regulatory side speaks for itself. There are [settlements](https://www.csbs.org/newsroom/state-regulators-issue-80-million-penalty-block-inc-cash-app-bsaaml-violations). There are [consent orders](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/enforcement/actions/block-inc/). The penalties are substantial. The public-facing pattern, which I think is industry-wide and not specific to any single employer, is that compliance work at fintech companies in growth phases is structurally undervalued: treated as a cost center, deprioritized when capital allocations get tight, asked to do more with less while the rest of the organization is told to ship faster.

I am proud of the work I did. I think it mattered. I am also uncertain about the larger institutional question that sits behind that work. How an organization committed in principle to financial inclusion ended up producing the regulatory record it did, and what that says about how principles get operationalized when capital pressures push back. The pride and the uncertainty are not in tension; they are the actual shape of having been there.

What I notice, reading the public coverage of fintech regulatory action across the industry, is that the structural pattern recurs everywhere. It is not about bad people in any specific firm. It is about a product space where the upside of speed is felt by capital and the downside of corner-cutting is absorbed by the most vulnerable. Compliance, when it is doing its job, is friction. Friction is expensive. The systems we have built around fintech have a strong, structural disposition to under-resource the people whose job is to add it. That is a fact about institutions, not a fact about individuals. It would be more comfortable to make it a fact about individuals, which is part of why I distrust the impulse. The same framework, applied to me, asks whether the voice I exercised was as loud as the moment required, and whether I left when I did for the right reasons rather than just for available ones. I do not have stable answers to either question.

## The politics of building

My political commitments are easy to state and harder to live by. What I care about most is whether the political and economic structures we have actually advance human flourishing. I do not think markets are inherently virtuous. I do not think they are inherently corrosive. I think they are tools whose effects are entirely contingent on the institutions surrounding them. One of the most important political questions of our time is whether the institutions we have are structurally responsive to stakeholders in the broader community, or only to capital and shareholders. My honest assessment, after nearly a decade in this industry, is that the answer is closer to the latter, and that this is not an accident.

What follows from this is not what some populist critics of new technology would have me conclude. There is a version of the political conversation, especially loud now about AI, in which an entire technological field gets cast as categorically harmful. I cannot endorse that version, and I want to be specific about why. AI is a subfield of computer science whose formal core is mathematics. To say that AI is bad is as insightful a statement as to say that linear regression is bad, or that molecular biology is bad. *y = mx + b* is not inherently good or bad. Neither is a deep neural network. Categorical ethical judgments of technological fields are usually too coarse to be useful, in computer science and outside it. The interesting questions are not at the field level. They are at the level of specific applications, deployments, and institutional contexts.

But the framing is not really about the math. It is a stand-in for an entirely reasonable feeling: that the people being affected by these technologies are not the people deciding how they get built or where they get pointed. The complaint is not against the equations. It is against the leveraging: against the gap between the technologists who choose what to build, the capital that funds them, and the everyone-else who absorbs the consequences. To mistake that gap for a disagreement about the math is what produces the bad version of the conversation. To own that it is a disagreement about institutions, about who has a say in deployment and to what ends, is what produces the version worth having.

Even the technologists, in practice, choose less freely than the framing implies. "Technologists who choose what to build" is true at the level of who writes the code; it is less true at the level of what gets built.

There is a useful comparison here to the film *Oppenheimer*, which I found powerful for exactly this reason. The physicists were not wrong that the underlying physics was real and beautiful and worth understanding. They were also not wrong, the ones who came to bristle at the nuclear weapons they had built, that the institutional shape of how the work got leveraged was something they had been naive about. The technical achievement and the leveraging of the technical achievement are not the same question. They also cannot be fully separated. You did not get to be a serious physicist of the 1940s without an answer to what should be done with what you had made. I do not get to be a serious technologist of the 2020s without an answer either.

The benefits of the technologies we are building are real. [AlphaFold](https://deepmind.google/science/alphafold/) is a contribution to biology that previous generations would have considered miraculous. Tools that allow a person with no formal training to write code, to make a piece of music, to draft a contract, to learn a language at conversational pace: these are not nothing. They are, in their best moments, a real broadening of who gets to participate. And the joy of working at the frontier of machine intelligence is real. It is the joy of being close to a serious answer to the question [Turing](https://courses.cs.umbc.edu/471/papers/turing.pdf) posed: can a machine think?

The harms are also real. The same technologies, deployed without serious attention to their institutional context, can hollow out the labor market for the displaced while making the displacement invisible to those it does not touch. Training data and design assumptions can carry forward biases hard to detect from inside the systems perpetuating them. The cognitive capacities that come from sitting with hard problems can languish if we offload too many of them to a machine willing to answer instantly. The information environment, already under strain, can degrade further as the costs of generating plausible content collapse. None of this means we should not build. It means we should build with the seriousness the technology warrants, which is a great deal of seriousness.

## What I am left with

Not a conclusion. A way of staying alert at my desk: to what I am building, who it is for, who it is not for, what it makes more possible and what it forecloses. Alert, too, to the gaps in my own seeing, which I know are there because I have caught myself in them, late, more than once.

Going forward, the question of where to lend my skills and my hours is not, for me, primarily a technical or financial one. It is a choice about which institutional orientation I want my work entangled with, and about what kinds of voice and exit will be available to me when I notice, as I will notice, that the orientation does not quite match what I was told. There is no clean institution. There are institutions whose constitutive pressures push in directions I can mostly endorse, and institutions whose pressures push in directions I cannot, and the difference between them is a thing it is my job to perceive and not a thing anyone else will perceive for me.

I have started spending real time on the leadership patterns at the companies I am considering. If I am going to do AI work, I will not join a place where safety researchers leave in waves, where pressing the edges of regulation is treated as competitive advantage, or where the company's political arm exists primarily to lobby against the rules the technology obviously needs. These are not abstract preferences. They are filters that have already cost me places I would otherwise have applied to.

I want to become the kind of person who can perceive the morally relevant aspects of my work, tell the truth about what I see, and act with courage when the institution makes that difficult. That is not the kind of thing you achieve. It is the kind of thing you practice, mostly fail at, and return to. This is an exercise in holding complexity.
